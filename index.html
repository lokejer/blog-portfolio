What to expect: SP DAAA

Comparisons with secondary school life, tips to succeed and more.

How/what you will learn

Is poly really "ownself settle"?

Everyone should be familiar with Student Learning Space (SLS). Polytechnics use something similar, a platform named Brightspace where you can access your class notes, online lectures and even weekly quizzes.
For those who prefer physical lectures (like myself), you'd be happy to know that not many modules rely heavily on online learning. Conceptually-heavy modules like Fundamentals of Programming (FOP) and Fundamentals of Computing (FOC) - the tougher modules to grasp - are taught in class.
Note that Weekly Quizzes, though located online, are conducted physically during class -- no cheating!

Typically, you'd read through the lesson slides before class to "prime your brain", so that you are better able to absorb concepts during physical class.

Hands-on Learning

The most common perspective of Polytechnics is they emphasise heavily on "hands-on learning". This is partly true - memorising concepts in a context-driven, application-heavy module like FOP won't get you anywhere near an 'A'. However for DAAA, I'd argue that modules like Math and FOC were quite similar to O-level E-Maths and Biology respectively, which helped ease my transition to polytechnic.

Summary

Staying disciplined by actively pre-reading class notes, keeping up with hands-on programming practice and revising for weekly quizzes are essential if you want to secure a good GPA.
Remember: It's a marathon, not a sprint.

Course Difficulty: FAQ

Is there a bell curve?

Most modules have a bell curve to regulate the number of 'A' students (usually 20-25%).
This means for a class of 20, only up to 5 students can achieve an 'A' grade.
Staying ahead of the curve is your responsibility - extra efforts like adding more unique features to your website or proactively consulting lecturers are important!

Are lecturers biased?

There isn't a definitive answer to this question. After all, we are humans with prejudices - intentional or not.
You can mitigate the chance of unfair treatment by simply not being obnoxious or overly-confident both in and outside of class.
Personally, i've met many Year 1s with heaps of programming experience completely disregard the teacher during class. Showing your competence is, ironically, the best way to get on their bad side.

How is the class culture like?

Every class is different, and this is something entirely out of your control.
I believe most students in DAAA did well in their O-level and chose this course with intent and purpose - what this means for you is less dead weights during groupwork and friends you can rely on!
Finally, focusing on things within your control is what matters most. Attending School of Computing's Freshman Orientation Camp held in early April is a way for you to build valuable friendships early on. Who knows? These friends might even become your classmates! (i'm speaking from experience)
